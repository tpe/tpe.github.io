Investors Tricks 2

Sample Chapter

It’s my job to invest on the best possible terms. In fact, I have a fiduciary duty to invest well. Remember, most VC and PE money comes from institutions. Those institutions give me money according to a mandate. And that mandate demands I turn straw into gold by investing in you.

While I have a fiduciary duty to my investors, I don’t have duties to you as a founder. Sure, it’s in my best interests to do well by you, but don’t forget where my loyalties lie. This isn’t some sleazy injustice, it’s just the way things are. Don’t fight it, just level the playing field by learning the tricks of the trade.

Investor Tricks #1 to #4

In Part 1 (Investor Tricks #1 to #4), I spoke about tactics used by investors to create the perception of closing the value gap. That means, we investors create the illusion that we’re meeting your price when in fact we’re engineering the term sheet to manipulate your perception.

In this article, Part 2, I’ll delve even further into these term-sheet tricks.

Why Reveal these Secrets?

I’m not writing this to antagonize founders. Quite the opposite. I want to prepare you to talk with investors one-on-one. I don’t want lawyers getting their sticky fingers on our relationship. Sure, you should absolutely confirm terms with a lawyer, but let’s not get into a fee-frenzy where our lawyers justify their existence at our expense. Apart from cost, we also start off on the wrong foot.

After I invest in a great founder, my #1 objective is to become part of the team. When you have a problem, I want you to call me first without even taking a breath. We’re in this together. Tricky term sheets, adversarial lawyers, and asymmetric information only attempt to destabilize our common objective.

Now, let’s get back to spilling the beans.

Trick #5: Employee Stock Options

You want me to invest $5 million at a $10 million valuation, but I want to invest for much less.

Okay, I’ll meet your expectation and invest $5 million at a pre-money valuation of $10 million, which includes a stock option pool (25% of the post-money value). We’ll use the option pool to build a superstar team and achieve world domination. Sound good?

See what’s happening here? The first trick is including the option pool in pre-money, while the second trick is using a percentage of the (higher) post-money value. Neither makes sense. It means you’re paying for future growth with value you created in the past (pre-money).

It’s not just 25% of $10 million, but 25% of $15 million. Except it comes out of pre-money.

The real pre-money is now $6.25 million (10 – (10+5) x 25%).

Trick #6: Investor Preferred Stock

Using the same example from above, you want $5 million at $10 million pre-money.

Okay, I’ll meet your valuation because I really believe in you. So I’ll invest at $10 million pre-money buying $5 million of preferred stock. Also, because there’s information asymmetry, and you have more control over the business, I want a 3x preference multiple and coupon of 12% pa. Think of it as an insurance premium.

First off, preferred subordination doesn’t just matter in bankruptcy. If you only manage to sell the business down the track for say $5 million, you get NOTHING! Subordination means I get my full investment back before you get anything. But, a 3x preference multiple means I have an insurance policy to receive at least 3x my investment. So, even if you sell for $15 million, I’m entitled to 3x my $5 million investment, so again, you get nothing.

Secondly, the preferred coupon represents a repayment of my investment. It’s essentially an ongoing transfer of value from you to me. In this example, the business must pay me $600k per year. This has significant value in today’s money.

In this example, you pay me a huge salary for the privilege of losing your business.

Trick #7: Management Fees

Let’s continue with the same example, $5 million at $10 million pre-money.

Okay, I’ll meet your expectation … so I’ll invest $5 million as preferred stock. Since I bring strategic experience and insight to the team, should be paid a salary like you, so there’s a management fee of $250k per quarter. That’s very cheap given my team’s deep experience. Plus, it further aligns our interests.

This is the same deal as the coupon, the management fee helps repay the investment. Instead of the coupon at $600k per year, my team receives a “salary” of 4 x $250k, which equals $1 million per year. My entire investment is repaid in 5 years and if we only sell for $5 million, it’s still all mine because my pref stock subordinates your ordinary stock.

Trick #8: Putting it all together

Same example.

Okay, okay, okay. I love what you’re doing and think you have an A-class team. I really believe in the business, so let’s do it.

Forget my $5 mill pre-money, I’ll agree to your $10 mill. A couple of conditions though. Like always, I will have preferred stock with a coupon of 15%. Since we’re agreeing to your valuation based on your confidence of next year’s earnings being $3 million, we have a ratchet that transfers 1% of equity per $10k below the forecast.

As mentioned, we also have lots of experience in this industry and excellent contacts. Since we’ll be working hard alongside you, we need a salary in the form of a management fee, which is $250k per quarter. We’ll even agree to the same board representation, but we want to appoint an additional independent chairperson.

Firstly, I know what you’re thinking, this sounds outrageous, it’s not at all realistic. Well, sorry to break it to you, but this is what MOST of our deals look like. Yes, MOST.

Let’s go through it in point form:

  • The pref stock means unless you increase value, you’ll be diluted big time.
  • The pref coupon means the company is paying back my investment, while you get nothing.
  • The management fee does the same, but increases repayments to $1.75 million/year!
  • My investment is repaid in ~3 years, but you cannot take a cent (aligned interests, etc).
  • The ratchet means that unless you produce a miracle and hit earnings of $3 million, you’ve lost a part (or all) of your business (see Part 1).
  • The appointment of the independent director tips the board in my favour. Independent? Ha!

Again, this is what MOST of our deals look like. In fact, there’s much more once you get into the finer detail. I explain all of those details in the eBook.

As I mentioned in Part 1, don’t assume you’re too smart to fall for this. I’m using obvious language here and we do this all day every day. Even your lawyers will miss our tricks.

But, Let’s Remember Something…

Term sheet negotiations aren’t about what’s right and wrong. They’re about a business transaction driven by many interconnected levers. If you pull a lever that’s important to you (e.g. a right to appoint the CEO), then you must expect me to pull a lever that’s important to me (e.g. an equity ratchet) in the opposite direction.

In part, we have these crazy structures because founders read too much news about Groupon et al. with billion-dollar valuations on unstable earnings. Of course, that’s not the whole story. The investing game is largely about manipulation and under-handed trickery. I want to undo it all and get back to open, upfront and forthcoming discussions.

As a founder, the most important thing you can do is understand the tricks of the investment trade. Don’t leave it up to a lawyer, or God forbid, the investor. Be smart and take care of your business.
